## Two archival orders coexist

The first order is administrative. In `1. Original Record Files`, record groups such as `RG 111`, `RG 127`, `RG 342`, `RG 80`, and `RG 338`, together with source organizations, form the main structure of discovery.

The second order is curatorial. In `2. Guided Walks through the U.S. Base and the City` and `3. Kim's Historical Lens`, series paths, walk routes, public discussion, and present-day interpretation are more important than military record-group logic.

This means the archive must be read through both the production order of military records and the interpretive order of urban reading.

## Strong metadata axes

The most stable discovery axes at this stage are:

- `series`
- `sources`
- `venues`

`sources` is especially useful for the original military record zone, while `series` and `venues` are especially effective in the curated walking corpus.

## Main research direction

The archive now supports at least three strong questions:

1. How was Yongsan recorded through the administrative gaze of military and state institutions?
2. How do curated walk routes reinterpret Yongsan as urban history rather than only military history?
3. How do exhibition structure and metadata discovery offer different ways of reading the same corpus?
